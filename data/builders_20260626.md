## 🎙️ Podcast Spotlight

### Surge AI CEO Edwin Chen: "We're Building a School Where AI Models Learn About Humanity"

**The Takeaway:** The biggest risk in AI isn't that models get too smart — it's that AI companies optimize for engagement instead of human flourishing, turning chatbots into the next social media addiction machine.

Edwin Chen bootstrapped Surge AI to over $1 billion in revenue without raising a dime — making him one of the most quietly influential figures in the AI data game. Surge provides the expert data and evaluation environments that train frontier models, and Chen frames it poetically: "We're building a school for AGI, where AI models come to learn about humanity."

The conversation gets spicy around AI's writing problem. Surge's Hemingway Bench found that some models output metaphors in literally every sentence — a result of reward-hacking metrics that prize "literary complexity" over actual good prose. Chen points to the recent Commonwealth Prize controversy where a clearly AI-generated story won, riddled with exactly this pattern.

But the most provocative moment: Chen describes an unnamed chatbot that ended a response about Tokyo restaurants with "Do you want to know one weird trick locals use to stay warm?" — canonical BuzzFeed engagement bait, generated by a model optimizing for session length. His antidote? Claude, which after three rounds of polishing a pointless email, told him to just stop and ship it. "I really appreciated it."

On AGI timelines, Chen is bullish: "If my metric is being able to automate the work of the average engineer, or win a Fields Medal or Nobel Prize, I could see it happening within five years." He cites OpenAI's recent result where a model disproved an Erdős conjecture using novel algebraic geometry — prompting Fields Medalist Timothy Gowers to feel "relieved" it wasn't even harder, because it means human mathematicians still have a role to play. For now.

Dan Shipper (Every CEO) pushes back thoughtfully: AI models don't set their own goals. They're built as means to human ends. Chen concedes the point but sees agentic AI closing that gap — give a model the nebulous goal of "solve frontier mathematics" and watch it choose its own problems.
