### 1. Anthropic Engineering: An Update on Recent Claude Code Quality Reports

Anthropic published a detailed postmortem on why Claude Code felt "dumber" to some users over the past month. Three separate bugs were identified and fixed:

- **March 4**: Default reasoning effort was dropped from "high" to "medium" to reduce latency — users noticed lower intelligence. Reverted April 7.
- **March 26**: A caching optimization had a bug that cleared Claude's thinking history on every turn after a session went idle for an hour, making Claude "forgetful." Fixed April 10.
- **April 16**: A system prompt change ("keep text between tool calls to ≤25 words") hurt coding quality. Reverted April 20.

The compound effect of three overlapping issues made it look like broad degradation. Anthropic is resetting usage limits for all subscribers as compensation. Going forward: internal staff will use the exact public build, broader eval suites will gate every system prompt change, and soak periods + gradual rollouts will be standard.

**Key quote**: "We never intentionally degrade our models." The transparency here is notable — few companies publish this level of detail on internal product bugs.

### 2. Anthropic Engineering: Scaling Managed Agents — Decoupling the Brain from the Hands

Anthropic's engineering team explains the architecture behind Claude Managed Agents. The core insight: decouple the "brain" (Claude + harness) from the "hands" (sandboxes/tools) and the "session" (event log). This mirrors how operating systems virtualized hardware into abstractions that outlast any specific implementation.

**Key design decisions**:
- Containers become "cattle not pets" — if one dies, the harness catches the error and Claude can retry with a new container
- Security: credentials never enter the sandbox where Claude's generated code runs (stored in vault, injected via proxy)
- Session log lives outside context window — enabling recovery from harness crashes and context management across long tasks
- Result: p50 TTFT dropped ~60%, p95 dropped over 90%

The "many brains, many hands" architecture means Claude can orchestrate multiple execution environments, pass sandboxes between agents, and connect to customer VPCs without network peering.

🔗 https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/managed-agents

Anthropic工程团队阐述了Claude托管智能体的架构设计。核心理念：将"大脑"（Claude+编排层）与"双手"（沙箱/工具）和"会话"（事件日志）解耦——借鉴操作系统将硬件虚拟化为持久抽象的设计哲学。关键成果包括：容器成为"牛群"而非"宠物"（崩溃可自动恢复）、凭证永不进入执行沙箱（安全隔离）、会话日志独立于上下文窗口存储（支持长任务恢复）。实测p50首Token延迟降低约60%，p95降低超90%。

### 3. Claude Blog: New in Claude Managed Agents — Self-Hosted Sandboxes and MCP Tunnels

Claude Managed Agents now supports running in sandboxes you control and connecting to your private MCP servers. Two new capabilities:

- **Self-hosted sandboxes** (public beta): Agent execution moves to your infrastructure while orchestration stays on Anthropic's side. Supported providers: Cloudflare, Daytona, Modal, Vercel. Sensitive files and repos never leave your perimeter.
- **MCP tunnels** (research preview): Agents reach MCP servers inside your private network via a lightweight outbound-only gateway — no inbound firewall rules, no public endpoints, traffic encrypted end-to-end.

Real customers already building: Amplitude (design agent on Cloudflare), Clay's Sculptor (GTM agent on Daytona), Rogo (finance analyst agent on Vercel Sandbox).

### AI & I by Every: "We Automated Everything With AI and Tripled Our Headcount"

**The Takeaway**: More AI automation creates *more* demand for human experts, not less — because AI floods the zone with "kind of right" work that needs human judgment to finish.

Dan Shipper (CEO of Every, a 30-person AI-native media company) shares a counterintuitive observation: despite using Claude Code, Codex, and AI agents across every role — "if you swing a stick around in our Slack, you're as likely to hit a human as you are an agent" — the company has grown from 4 to 30 people since GPT-3 launched, and is still hiring.

His thesis ("After Automation"):
1. AI makes yesterday's expert competence cheap — anyone can now write code, design, or draft reports
2. This floods the zone with work that's "close but not quite right"
3. That *increases* demand for experts who can build systems to get AI-generated work across the finish line, and who can use AI to create things that were previously impossible
4. "The further away an agent gets from a human, the less valuable it is"

On the ClickUp CEO who fired 22% of his workforce: Dan argues most companies implementing AI poorly will boomerang — "two months later, they're like, oops, can you come back?" He also notes that customer service centers are finding customers actively trying to detect and bypass AI to reach humans.

On AGI: Dan defines it as "any agent you never turn off — that it makes economic sense to keep running all the time, actively generating tokens." He sees the entire AI industry structurally incentivized toward compliance and human direction, not autonomy.

**Best quote**: "You prompt AI to do something. It blows your mind. You feel inadequate. You feel like, oh my god, this thing's gonna take my job. And then it stops working, it looks back at you and says, what should I do next?"
